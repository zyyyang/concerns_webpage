What you may be experiencing

Your hair feels rough, tangles, and breaks before it ever gets longer.

Dry-looking ends. Knots that catch when you comb. Short broken pieces that were never cut. Hair that looks untidy again within minutes of stepping outside. For many women the change becomes obvious after bleaching, colouring, rebonding, perming, a smoothing or keratin treatment, or a long stretch of heat styling. Every product since has helped for a week and then worn off.

The assumption underneath most of this is that damaged hair can be repaired back to health with the right treatment. The honest picture is harder, and more useful.

An already-grown strand is not living tissue and cannot heal itself. Severe chemical damage is permanent. It cannot be reversed, by us or by anyone else, and the fastest route back to healthy hair is to remove what is destroyed and protect what grows in behind it.

What’s happening

This is the one concern in the set about the hair shaft rather than the follicle or the scalp. Each strand has an outer cuticle protecting an inner cortex, which carries most of its strength. Bleaching, permanent colour, rebonding, perming, smoothing and keratin services, high heat, and repeated friction can chip or strip away cuticle cells and, when the exposure is heavy enough, reach the cortex beneath. As the surface roughens, strands catch on one another. They tangle, feel coarse, reflect less light, and become easier to split and snap.

Breakage is not hair loss. A broken hair snaps somewhere along its length. A hair lost from the root comes away whole, often with a small pale bulb. They have different causes and different answers, and the two can happen at the same time.

The severity depends on what the hair had already been through. A chemical service that is safe on virgin hair can be destructive on hair that has been bleached, and the worst outcomes usually come from services applied on top of one another: bleach followed by rebonding, a perm on lightened hair, a smoothing treatment on hair that was already compromised. In the most severe cases the fibre is destroyed outright and the hair does not merely feel damaged, it disintegrates. This is not common, but it is not theoretical either, and it cannot be undone.

Singapore’s humidity plays a smaller part than it is usually given credit for. Hair exchanges water with the air, and in humid conditions that disrupts the temporary bonds holding a blow-dry or a straightened style in place. Hair that is naturally wavy or curly, or made more porous by chemical processing, therefore frizzes and loses smoothness faster here. Humidity makes existing damage and natural texture more visible. What follows it does the real harm: reaching for high heat again to flatten the frizz, or dragging a brush through hair that has knotted.

Most products that improve damaged hair do so by reducing friction, smoothing the surface, and helping strands slide past one another instead of catching. That is a real benefit and worth having. It is not the same as rebuilding the strand. A heavier mask is not automatically a better one, and there is no universal ratio of protein to moisture that can be prescribed by looking at your hair. Judge a product by whether the hair detangles more easily, stays flexible rather than brittle, and leaves fewer short broken pieces behind.

Bond repair deserves a word of its own, because the term is on every shelf. It is not one technology. Some products penetrate the fibre and reinforce chemical interactions inside damaged hair. Others mainly condition the surface or lay down a protective film. The evidence, the mechanism, and the durability differ from product to product, so the phrase on the bottle does not prove that broken bonds have been restored. A good one can genuinely improve strength, flexibility and resistance to breakage. None of them returns severely damaged hair to its original structure.

Leonica’s diagnostic lens

Severe chemical damage cannot be reversed. What can be decided is what happens next, and the most consequential decision is whether your hair can safely take another chemical service. The salon proposing that service is not a neutral party to the question. Hairstylists are trained in cutting, colour and technique rather than in the biology of the fibre, and the person selling the treatment has no commercial reason to tell you your hair cannot withstand it. Frequently asked questions
Can damaged hair be repaired?

Improved, yes. Repaired, not in the way the word suggests. An already-grown strand is not living tissue and cannot heal itself. Its softness, strength and manageability can be improved, and further breakage prevented. But severely processed lengths cannot be returned to their original condition, and the only way to replace them is to grow new hair and protect it as it grows.

Why does my hair break instead of growing longer?

Because it is growing and breaking at roughly the same rate. The hair is still growing from the scalp, but the lengths are snapping at their weakest points before they gain any visible length. This is why hair can seem to stall at the same length for years, and why cutting off the damaged portion sometimes produces more length rather than less.

Should I cut it off?

Usually, yes, at least the worst of it. Once the fibre is destroyed there is nothing to recover, and hair that is splitting and snapping will keep breaking further up the shaft as you brush and style it. Removing the damaged portion is the fastest route to hair that looks and behaves well, and it lets you put your effort into protecting the healthy hair growing in behind it. A salon is the right place for the haircut. It is not the right place to be talked into another chemical service on the way out.

Can I have another treatment to fix it?

This is the question worth pausing on. A chemical service that is safe on healthy hair can be destructive on hair that has already been processed, and the worst damage usually comes from services layered on top of one another. A salon has every reason to say yes. Before you agree to a smoothing treatment, a perm, a rebond or more bleach on hair that is already compromised, it is worth having someone with no stake in the sale tell you whether your hair can take it.

Is breakage the same as hair loss?

No, and it matters which one you have. Breakage snaps the shaft along its length and leaves short pieces. Hair loss releases the whole hair from the root, often with a small pale bulb. One strand is not enough to tell, so look at the overall picture. Both can happen at once, which is one of the more common reasons to have it looked at.

Is all frizz a sign of damage?

No. Naturally wavy, curly or coarse hair frizzes in humidity without being damaged at all, especially if it is being brushed and styled as though it were straight. Frizz on its own is not a diagnosis. Frizz alongside snapping, short broken pieces and rough, tangling ends is a different picture.

Do bond-repair products actually work?

Some do meaningful work. The difficulty is that bond repair is a marketing category rather than a single technology, so the phrase on the bottle tells you little. Some products penetrate the fibre and reinforce it. Others are effective conditioners in different packaging. The evidence differs product by product. A good one can improve strength and reduce breakage. None will make bleached hair unbleached, and continued processing or high heat will overwhelm any benefit.
