What you may be experiencing

Most men notice it in a photograph before they notice it in the mirror.

A hairline that has crept back. A crown that shows more scalp under bright light. Hair that no longer styles the way it used to. The change is gradual, which is part of what makes it unsettling, because by the time it is obvious it has usually been happening for a while.

You may already be weighing up a transplant, or working through tonics and supplements, or have been told there is nothing to do until you are ready for surgery. The advice tends to jump straight to procedures, and rarely begins with what is actually happening on your scalp.

A follicle that is thinning is not a follicle that is lost. Much of male pattern hair loss is miniaturisation in progress, and how much of it can still be influenced depends heavily on how early it is addressed.

What’s happening

Male pattern hair loss is driven by genetics and by dihydrotestosterone, or DHT, which is produced from testosterone by an enzyme called 5-alpha reductase. In men who are genetically susceptible, the hair follicles are sensitive to DHT, and that sensitivity can be inherited from either parent.

When DHT binds to a receptor in a sensitive follicle, it sets off a process called miniaturisation. With each growth cycle the follicle shrinks a little, the hair it produces becomes finer, shorter and lighter, and the active growth phase shortens. Over time the follicle produces only thin, wispy hair, and may eventually stop producing visible hair at all. It can begin as early as puberty, in some cases from around the age of sixteen.

The follicles most sensitive to DHT sit at the temples and across the top of the head, while those at the back and sides are largely resistant to it. This is why most men lose hair in a similar and predictable pattern, commonly described using the Norwood Scale, rather than losing it evenly across the scalp.

Everyone loses hair daily and grows it back. Here the follicle itself is shrinking, so what is lost is replaced by something finer, and eventually by nothing. That is the difference, and it is why this does not settle on its own the way a temporary bout of hair fall does.

Leonica’s diagnostic lens

Male pattern hair loss is common, but how far it has progressed, how quickly it is moving, and what else is compounding it differ from one man to another. The assessment answers those questions before any plan is made.

01
The pattern and stage of loss, mapped across the hairline, temples, and crown, and how it has changed over time
02
Hair shaft calibre and the proportion of miniaturised hairs, which indicates how much of the loss is still recoverable and how much is not
03
Family history and age of onset, which inform how aggressively the condition is likely to progress
04
Scalp condition, including inflammation and sebum balance, since an inflamed scalp is a poorer environment for the follicles that remain
05
Previous treatments and procedures, and any relevant blood work, so the plan builds on what is already known rather than starting again

The aim is an honest picture: what can realistically be preserved, what can be improved, and what each option can and cannot achieve. No single intervention resolves male pattern hair loss permanently, and any plan that suggests otherwise is worth treating with suspicion.

Frequently asked questions
Can male pattern hair loss be reversed?

Not reversed, but often slowed, and partly recovered where the follicles are still active. Follicles that are miniaturising can frequently be supported back toward a healthier calibre. Follicles that have been dormant for years are far harder to recover, and some will not return. This is the whole reason early assessment matters: the sooner it is addressed, the more there is left to work with.

Should I just get a hair transplant?

A transplant can be the right choice, but not on its own and often not yet. It moves hair. It does not stop the process that is thinning the rest. If the native hair around the grafts keeps receding, the result deteriorates even after a technically successful procedure. This is also why transplant surgeons commonly advise keeping the remaining hair stabilised afterwards. We further advise against rushing into a transplant while the loss is still active and in its early stages, because more of the surrounding hair is likely to go and the result can end up looking patchy.

Do I need medication?

Possibly, and it is not a question we can answer for you. Trichologists do not prescribe. Finasteride and minoxidil are medical decisions and belong with a doctor.

What is worth understanding is what each thing does. Medication acts on the hormonal driver, the DHT sensitivity that is shrinking the follicles. Scalp care acts on the environment those follicles are living in. They are not alternatives to one another, and neither replaces the other. A man taking medication is still better served by a scalp that is not inflamed. A man who declines it, which is a legitimate choice, should be clear that the underlying process continues regardless.

An assessment establishes how quickly the loss is moving and how much of it is still recoverable, which is the information worth having before that decision is made, whichever way it goes.

Is it too late if I have been losing hair for years?

Rarely entirely, though it may be too late for some of it. There is usually density still worth protecting, and the scalp environment can almost always be improved. What an assessment establishes is which of those two you are dealing with, rather than assuming either.

Will I definitely go bald if my father did?

Genetic susceptibility raises the likelihood, but the rate of progression varies considerably between individuals, and it can be influenced. Early and consistent care changes the trajectory for many men. It does not make the susceptibility go away.
