What you may be experiencing

Postpartum hair loss can feel far more alarming than expected.

The amount of hair left on the pillow, in the shower, or in your hands can be deeply unsettling, especially when you are already recovering from childbirth and adjusting to the demands of caring for a baby.

You may have been told that it is normal and that you simply have to wait it out. You may also have tried supplements, shampoos, tonics, or popular hair growth recommendations, only to feel unsure whether anything is truly helping.

While postpartum hair fall is common, the frustration and uncertainty around it are very real. The question is not only whether the hair fall will eventually reduce, but whether your scalp, hair cycle, and overall recovery are being properly supported during this period.

What’s happening

During pregnancy, elevated oestrogen levels can keep more hair follicles in the active growth phase for longer. This is why many women notice their hair becoming fuller or thicker during pregnancy.

After delivery, oestrogen levels drop. Hair that remained in the growth phase for longer during pregnancy may then shift out of that phase around the same period, resulting in heavier hair fall in the months after childbirth. This is a recognised biological response, not a sign that something has gone wrong.

Two things are often confused here. The heavy hair fall settling and the lost density returning are separate events, on separate timelines.

For most women the hair fall does return to its usual daily level. Regrowing what was lost is a slower and less certain process, and what happens during that period matters. Nutritional depletion, interrupted sleep, stress, and the increased physical demands placed on the body during recovery and breastfeeding all influence how well the hair cycle recovers, which is why it is worth looking closely at those factors while the hair is regrowing rather than after.

Frequently asked questions
How long does postpartum hair loss usually last?

Postpartum hair fall commonly begins a few months after delivery and, for most women, returns to a normal daily level over the following months. That is what most people mean when they say it has stopped. It is not the same as the hair having grown back. Regrowth is a separate and slower process, and your previous density is not restored simply because the hair fall has settled.

When should I be concerned about postpartum hair loss?

Hair fall that begins a few months after delivery is expected and usually settles back to its normal daily level. It is worth having it looked at sooner if the hair fall feels unusually heavy, if it continues past the point you expected it to settle, if your parting or ponytail looks visibly thinner, or if there is scalp irritation or patchy loss alongside it.

Waiting until the thinning is obvious is not the safer option. By the time density is visibly reduced, a meaningful amount has usually already gone, and the factors that commonly prolong postpartum hair fall, such as low iron, thyroid changes, and the nutritional and sleep demands of recovery, are easier to address while they are still active.

Can breastfeeding affect hair loss recovery?

Breastfeeding itself is not usually the only explanation, but it can be part of the wider recovery picture. Sleep disruption, nutritional demand, stress, diet, iron stores, and overall recovery after childbirth may all influence how quickly the hair cycle recovers.

Can low iron or ferritin make postpartum hair fall worse?

Low iron stores may contribute to prolonged hair fall or slower regrowth in some mothers. Where relevant, we may guide you on what blood work could be useful and how to interpret the results in relation to your hair and scalp condition.

Will my hair return to its previous thickness?

Often, but not automatically, and not simply because the hair fall has stopped. Once hair fall returns to its normal level, the follicles still have to regrow what was lost, and new hair grows at roughly a centimetre a month. Restored thickness therefore lags well behind settled hair fall. Many mothers do see their density return over the following year.

Recovery is less complete when something is limiting the follicle while it is trying to regrow: low iron stores, thyroid changes, low nutritional intake, sustained sleep debt and stress, or a scalp condition. Pregnancy can also bring forward a gradual thinning that was already beginning, in which case the density that does not return was never lost to the postpartum hair fall at all. This is the reason not to simply wait it out. The window in which those factors can be corrected is while the hair is regrowing, not after.
