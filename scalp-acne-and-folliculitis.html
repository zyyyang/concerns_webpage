What you may be experiencing

Small, sore bumps on your scalp that behave like acne but never quite clear.

Tender spots, pimples, or pustules across the scalp, often along the hairline, the crown, or the back of the head. They may itch, sting when you brush or wash, and flare after a workout, a hot day, or wearing a cap or helmet. In Singapore's heat and humidity, they can be relentless.

Treating them like facial acne, with scrubs and acne washes, often does little or makes them angrier.

What looks like scalp acne is usually folliculitis, an inflammation of the hair follicle. Knowing which kind you have changes the treatment entirely, which is why the usual acne products so often disappoint.

What’s happening

Folliculitis is inflammation of the hair follicle, and it has more than one cause. Some cases are bacterial. Very commonly in hot, humid climates, it is driven by Malassezia, a yeast that lives on every scalp and overgrows in sweat, oil, and trapped heat. This fungal type looks like acne but does not respond to acne treatment, which is the usual reason it keeps returning. Review: Confirm the bacterial vs Malassezia (fungal) folliculitis distinction and source it.

Sweat, heat, oil, and anything that traps them against the scalp, caps, helmets, long humid days, intense exercise, all feed the cycle. This is why it is so common in Singapore.

True acne, seborrheic dermatitis, and folliculitis can look similar and sometimes occur together, which makes self-treatment hit or miss. Most folliculitis is manageable once the type is identified and the triggers addressed. Persistent or severe inflammation, though, can affect the follicle itself, and in its scarring forms it can threaten the hair in that area, which is why it is worth taking seriously rather than waiting out. Review: Confirm scarring folliculitis can cause hair loss in the affected area; source it.

Frequently asked questions
Is scalp acne the same as face acne?
Usually not. Most scalp breakouts are folliculitis, an inflammation of the follicle that is often bacterial or, in humid climates, fungal. Because the cause differs from facial acne, facial acne products frequently fail to clear it.
Why do my scalp bumps keep coming back?
Recurrence usually means the underlying type has not been correctly identified, or the triggers, sweat, oil, heat, and occlusion, have not been addressed. Fungal folliculitis in particular returns when treated as ordinary acne.
Can scalp folliculitis cause hair loss?
Most cases do not. Persistent or severe inflammation can affect the follicle, and certain scarring forms can lead to hair loss in the affected area, which is why ongoing or worsening cases should be assessed.
Does sweating cause it?
Sweat itself does not, but heat, sweat, and trapped moisture create the environment in which it thrives. This is why it is so common here and often flares after exercise or under caps and helmets.
Can I use my facial acne products on my scalp?
Often they are not the right fit, especially if the cause is fungal rather than bacterial, and they can sometimes irritate the scalp further. Identifying the type first means whatever you use actually targets the cause.
